By Shahid Rahman  ·  Inaugural Essay

We have long confused the appearance of order for the substance of authority. When a state compels compliance through fear, it does not govern — it negotiates, constantly, with the terms of its own fragility. This essay argues that coerced obedience is not stable authority at all, but something else entirely: managed resistance.

"Power does not fail when people disobey. Power fails when the people obey only because they are forced to."

The resilience of a political order depends not on the volume of its commands but on the quality of the obedience they produce. A regime that rules through coercion alone is perpetually one crisis away from collapse — each act of forced compliance a reminder of the distance between authority and legitimacy.

A Critique on the Anatomy of Obedience

On coercion, legitimacy, and the kind of compliance that keeps states alive

By Shahid Rahman · Inaugural Essay · 5 March 2026

Power does not fail when people disobey.
Power fails when the people obey only because they are forced to.

A system sustained by coercion alone should be described not as a stable authority but as a managed resistance. Some of these states will collapse. Others will endure. But the operative question is never whether compliance exists — it is what kind of compliance it is. Obedience is not binary. It is a spectrum, ranging from the voluntary deference of a people who trust their state to the coerced compliance of a people who simply have no other choice. The stability of any political order depends, more than anything else, on which end of that spectrum its authority rests.

I.   What Obedience Is

Obedience is a form of compliance to authority. It does not necessarily mean automatic respect, belief, or agreement with a state's authority — it describes how behaviour is oriented under it. A citizen may despise the government and still pay taxes, observe curfews, refrain from violence. That is obedience. What distinguishes the kinds of obedience is not the behaviour itself, but the reasoning — or the fear — that produces it.

II.   Voluntary Obedience and Its Difficulty

Voluntary obedience is rooted in the legitimacy of the state and its leadership. When a people are voluntarily obedient, they respect the law not because they must but because they understand why it exists, and broadly accept that understanding. They internalise civic duty as something real, not merely imposed. This is the most stable form of authority — and, for any state already in power, one of the hardest to sustain.

The difficulty is structural. The people need a scapegoat for their grievances, and in a functioning democracy, the state is the most available one. Discontent accumulates. Opposition becomes more legible, more appealing. Voluntary obedience erodes not through dramatic rupture but through a slow withdrawal of faith.

States that cannot regenerate legitimacy will attempt to manufacture obedience through externalisation of threat.

When a government comes under heavy duress, the temptation is well-documented: make bold, patriotic statements; aim the public gaze toward a new target. A new war, conveniently unjust. Exploitation in a far-away land, where distance permits dehumanisation of its people and its leaders. Or simply, the adoption of a performative populist character. Demand radical — potentially authoritarian — policy change in that character, and the people will become self-policing, no longer willing to extend solidarity to their neighbours. The civic fabric frays precisely when it is most needed.

The preventive answer is not rhetoric but architecture. Institutional trust and civic values must be cultivated through education and a political culture that promotes shared responsibility over perpetual antagonism. A state oriented toward civic republicanism — one that genuinely invests in citizens' capacity for self-governance — is structurally better positioned to cultivate voluntary obedience without resorting to coercive maintenance. This is not idealism. It is a matter of long-term structural resilience.

III.   Coerced Obedience and Its Costs

Coerced obedience is no more created than it is removed. It is maintained — by the threat of authority, by the visible apparatus of enforcement, by the knowledge of what happens to those who step outside the permitted range of behaviour. That maintenance carries high costs and is profoundly brittle under crisis. It is characterised by the use of police as a weapon rather than a civic institution; by the surveillance of nearly every citizen; and in ostensibly democratic states, by the weaponisation of opposing parties' propaganda to keep populations in a state of managed anxiety.

What coercion produces is surface compliance. Beneath it, resistance accumulates — slowly, quietly, and with particular intensity when coercion is visited by blood. Overreliance on coercion does not stabilise a state. It accelerates the conditions for its decay.

IV.   The Spectrum and Its Stakes

Most political systems contain both types of obedience simultaneously. The relative balance between them determines not only day-to-day stability but the resilience of the state under pressure. Systems reliant on voluntary obedience can bend — they absorb shocks, adapt, recover. Systems reliant on coercion are rigid, and rigid things fracture. A crisis that a legitimate state weathers becomes, in a coercive state, the moment of collapse.

In most democratic states, parties spend considerable energy spreading contempt — for the opposition, for institutions, for the very idea of civic trust. This is a form of self-harm at the scale of a polity. The people and their representatives should work, however imperfectly, toward conditions in which shared governance is possible. This is not naivety about political conflict. It is a recognition that the alternative — a permanent war of all against all, conducted in legislative chambers and social media feeds — is precisely what coercive authority depends on to survive.

Conclusion

Legitimacy explains why people accept rule. Restraint explains how leaders should preserve it. Obedience shows how power is enacted. The three are not separable. A state that understands only coercion understands only one of them, and it will misread the others until it is too late. The anatomy of obedience is, in the end, the anatomy of authority itself — and what this examination reveals is that power sustained only by force was never truly held at all.
